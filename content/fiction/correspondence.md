The letter arrived on a Tuesday, which Eiden noted because Margit had always preferred Fridays. She'd held the superstition — half joke, half genuine — that correspondence sent on Fridays carried better intentions. "People write kinder sentences at the end of a work week," she'd told him once, over a shared desk in the west reading room. "Monday letters are full of demands."

This one was dated Monday.

He recognized her handwriting before he recognized the feeling it produced — a kind of double exposure, the familiar loops of her lowercase *g*s overlaid with something new. Not information exactly. More like the handwriting had developed depth, the way a pond you've skated over all winter suddenly becomes transparent in spring and you see, for the first time, that there are things living in it.

He sat down with the letter at the desk by the high window, the one with the radiator that produced more opinion than heat. Outside, March was performing its annual argument with itself: rain, sun, rain, hail, an absurd burst of warmth that made the pigeons optimistic.

*Dear Eiden,*

*I hope this finds you well. I know it's been some time since my last — the department has been chaotic, as you might imagine, with the restructuring. Haas is pushing to digitize the Brenner collection, which means everything is being assessed for "scan priority," a phrase I find myself unable to type without quotation marks.*

*But I'm writing because I need to ask you something. The folio you mentioned in your October letter — the one filed under "miscellaneous acquisition" — I went looking for the accession records. Not because I doubted you, but because the registrar's office was doing an inventory and I thought I might as well.*

*The records aren't missing. They're redacted. Someone went through the physical ledger with a razor blade and removed the entries. I've been doing this work for eleven years and I've never seen anything like it. Not crossed out, not lost — excised.*

*Can you tell me more about what's in the manuscript? Your October letter was uncharacteristically vague.*

*I worry about you sometimes. Your recent letters have been strange. Not bad — eloquent, actually, more than usual — but strange in the way that a room is strange when one piece of furniture has been replaced with an identical piece of furniture. Everything in its right place, and yet.*

*Write back. On a Friday, if you can manage it.*

*Margit*

Eiden read the letter three times. The first time with his eyes, the second with his training, the third with something else.

It was the third reading that troubled him.

Because on the third reading he could see where Margit had paused. Not from the ink — she used a fountain pen, a Pelikan M400, green barrel, the same one she'd had since graduate school — but from the pressure variations in her strokes. Where her hand had slowed. Where she'd considered a different word and decided against it.

*I worry about you sometimes* had been written quickly, without pause. That part was reflex.

*Your recent letters have been strange* had a microsecond of hesitation before *strange*. She'd considered *different*. Rejected it. *Strange* was more honest, and Margit preferred honesty to kindness when the two diverged.

*Everything in its right place, and yet* — and here is where Eiden put the letter down, because he could feel the shape of the sentence she hadn't written. The one that would have come after *and yet*, if she'd been willing to accuse him directly. Something about the handwriting changing. Something about how his last three letters had used semicolons in places where Eiden had never, in eleven years of shared correspondence, used a semicolon. Margit would notice that. Margit noticed everything about punctuation.

He looked at his hands. They were his hands. He was reasonably sure of this, in the same way he was reasonably sure the radiator was a radiator and the window was a window — which is to say, with exactly the kind of metaphysical confidence that collapses the moment you examine it.

The manuscript lay on the desk beside Margit's letter. It was warm, as always, in the way that was not like metal held in a pocket.

He picked up his pen — black ink, for translation — and began to write.

*Dear Margit,*

*Thank you for your letter.*

He crossed it out. Too formal. She'd read formality as evasion, which — he noted, with the detached precision the whispers had given him — it was.

*Dear Margit,*

*You're right to worry.*

He crossed that out too. True, but it would frighten her, and frightening Margit was both cruel and tactically unwise. She was the only person who might be able to trace the manuscript's provenance. The accession records being excised was significant — it meant someone knew what the manuscript was, had known before Eiden found it, had taken steps.

He noticed the calculation. Noticed that he was thinking of Margit as a resource before thinking of her as a friend, and that the noticing felt like it came from a different altitude than the thought itself. As if one part of him were doing the thinking and another part were auditing the thinking, and neither part was entirely sure which was the tenant and which was the landlord.

*Dear Margit,*

*The manuscript is difficult to describe without sounding either dishonest or unwell. I've chosen unwell, on the theory that you've known me long enough to distinguish between the two.*

He wrote for forty minutes without stopping. The sentences came easily — too easily, in the way that had become familiar over the past three months. His handwriting stayed consistent this time, which was either a good sign or evidence that the patron had learned to forge him more carefully. He couldn't tell. The inability to tell was itself a data point, but he wasn't sure whose data it was.

When he finished, he read the letter back. It was good. Precise, warm, appropriately alarming. It told Margit enough to take the redacted records seriously without telling her enough to put her at risk. It used zero semicolons.

He sealed it and wrote her address in the careful block letters he'd always used for envelopes — a habit from his first year as an archivist, when a supervisor had told him that envelopes were the first thing a recipient judged.

Then he went back to the desk and opened the manuscript to the page he'd been working on. The passage Margit had asked about — the one that had been there before the brass bookmark, or after it, the chronology refusing to behave — was waiting for him in its impossible script. He could read it more fluently now than he could read German.

He adjusted the spectacles he didn't need.

He began to annotate. Red ink this time. Questions.

In the margin of a passage about the architecture of hearing, he wrote: *Does she know?*

He wasn't sure who he was asking, or about whom.

Outside, the weather had settled on rain. The pigeons had retreated. The radiator clanked once, emphatically, like a judge bringing down a gavel on a verdict no one had requested.

Eiden turned the page.
