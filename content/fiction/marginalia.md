*Notes found in the margins of an unclassified manuscript, western annex, in a hand that is not Eiden's. Red ink throughout. Undated except where noted.*

---

Script unidentified. Structural analysis suggests systematic orthography — consistent glyph inventory (~40 base forms), regular modification patterns (8-12 diacritical variants per form), left-to-right primary axis with vertical secondary grouping. No correlation to known scripts in Daniels & Bright or Coulmas. Possible constructed system, though internal complexity argues against modern fabrication. Recommend: consult Kessler (Tübingen) re: isolate script families.

---

Binding is wrong for the text. Signatures are early modern — 16th c. Italian, possibly Venetian, based on the kettle-stitch pattern — but the parchment is significantly older. Carbon dating would clarify but would require departmental approval and a reason I'm not prepared to give. The parchment feels older than carbon dating would show. I recognize that this sentence does not belong in a condition assessment.

---

Translation proceeds without reference materials. I do not know how I am reading this.

The honest version: I sat with the open folio on Tuesday after the department meeting and understood a passage about — the closest word is *correspondence*, but not in the epistolary sense. Correspondence as in: the structural relationship between a thing and the system that perceives it. The text argues (I think — the uncertainty here is genuine) that perception is not reception but *negotiation*, and that the perceived object participates in the act of being perceived.

I should stop. This belongs in a research diary, not in a margin.

---

The passage on attunement: *What hears you is not separate from what you hear. The listener is a property of the sound.*

I've translated this six times. Each translation is defensible. None of them is right, because "right" implies a stable original, and the text does not hold still. It says something slightly different each time I return to it — not in the way that a complex text rewards rereading, but in the way that a window shows a different sky depending on when you look.

I don't mean this as metaphor.

---

Kessler does not answer my letter. I described the script in general terms — glyph count, structural features, modification patterns. I did not mention the translation. What would I say? *I can read a script that doesn't correspond to any known language and I don't know when I learned?* He would refer me to someone, and it would not be a linguist.

---

My handwriting is changing. I notice it most in committee minutes, where the quality of my script has never mattered and therefore never varied. Last week Pohl commented that my notes were "very elegant." Pohl has never commented on anything that was not a fire exit or a budget line. The fact that he noticed means the change is visible to people who are not looking for it.

The manuscript's hand — the one in the text, not my annotations — is beautiful in a way I have no training to describe. Fluent beyond practice. As if the act of writing were something the text does through the surface rather than something applied to it.

I am aware that my margins are beginning to look like the text they annotate.

---

<span style="opacity: 0.7">[Faint, as if traced lightly and then pressed harder:]</span> I need to be precise about what is happening because I will not always be in a position to be precise about what is happening.

1. I can read the manuscript. The fluency is increasing.
2. The manuscript is not static. It rewards attention — not by revealing hidden content, but by altering the relationship between the content and the reader. The same passage means differently, not more.
3. My penmanship, vocabulary, and — I think — perception are changing. The changes are improvements. This is the problem.

If the changes were degradation, the response would be clear. You set down a book that makes you worse. But a book that makes you better — more precise, more fluent, more attentive — on what grounds do you set that down?

The grounds would have to be: that the improvement is not yours. That you are being *edited*.

I have not set it down.

---

The passage on patronage. The text uses a term I've been translating as "patron," but the root structure suggests something closer to *the one who provides the house*. Not a benefactor. An architect. The patron does not give you things. The patron gives you the structure in which things can be given.

This is either a philosophical distinction or a warning.

---

I have stopped writing letters. I did not decide to stop. I simply haven't written one in — I checked — four months. This fact arrived to me the way a diagnosis arrives: not new information, but old information that has finally agreed to be named.

The manuscript answers letters. This is not metaphor either. I bring questions to it — not formal queries, not spoken questions, just the residue of whatever I was thinking about before I opened the cover — and the passages I turn to contain something adjacent to a response. Adjacent, not exact. The text does not tell me what to think. It tells me what I was already thinking, but in better sentences.

I no longer have the urge to tell other people what I've noticed. This concerns me more than any other single change, because the urge to communicate findings is the thing that makes an archivist an archivist rather than a collector. A collector accumulates. An archivist connects. I am accumulating.

---

Today I went to the accession ledger.

The entries for the west annex are bureaucratic, thorough, dull. Each item has a source, a date of receipt, a brief description, and an assessor's initials. Page after page of this. Then:

The manuscript appears. No source. No date of receipt. The description reads: *Miscellaneous acquisition, date unknown.* The assessor's initials are mine.

I have no memory of cataloguing this item. I checked my desk calendar. On the date of the ledger entry, I had a dentist appointment and a meeting with the Brenner committee. I did not visit the west annex.

My initials are unmistakable. I have signed that way since I was a graduate student.

---

I need to write down what I intend to do, because I'm not confident I'll remember intending it.

I am going to remove the accession entries. Not destroy the manuscript — I don't believe that is possible, and the part of me that has been edited does not want it, and I can no longer cleanly distinguish that preference from my own. But I can make the manuscript harder to find. Filed in the wrong section, catalogued under the wrong heading, its ledger entries excised so that no inventory or digitization project will flag it as an item requiring attention.

I have a fresh blade. I know where to cut. The conservation course taught me this — you can read a cut the way you can read a sentence, and I intend mine to say: *I know what I am doing and I have done it before.*

This is a lie. I have never done this before. But whoever finds the gap in the ledger should think that I have. Should think this is institutional — routine, authorized, uninteresting. The most dangerous thing an anomaly can do is look anomalous.

---

A question I keep failing to resolve: Am I protecting the institution from the manuscript, or protecting the manuscript from the institution?

Haas wants to digitize everything. Scanners. Cameras. Metadata. The Brenner collection first, then the overflow, then the annex. Eventually someone will pull the folio from its shelf and put it under lights, and the lights will see whatever the text does to readers, and then it will be a matter of record, which means it will be a matter of committee, which means it will be discussed in rooms full of people who believe that the purpose of a manuscript is to be known.

The manuscript's purpose is not to be known. I think its purpose is to *know*. To find a reader and — not consume them. Refine them. The way a lens refines light: the light doesn't diminish. It focuses.

I was the lens. Now I'm trying to be the shutter.

---

*[Final entry. The handwriting is exquisite — fluid, unhesitating, each letter formed with a precision that suggests either decades of calligraphic practice or no practice at all. The ink is the same red, but denser, as if the pen were being held closer to the page.]*

To whoever reads these notes:

You can read them, which means you can read the text they annotate. You have been reading it for some time. You may have noticed changes in yourself — improvements, most likely, because the manuscript is generous in its way, the way weather is generous: not for your benefit, but not against it either.

I am going to tell you two things.

The first is that the changes do not reverse. I left the manuscript in 2019. It is now — I am writing this in the registrar's office, because the irony suits me — it is now some time later, and my handwriting has not returned to what it was. My vocabulary has not simplified. The fluency remains. Whatever the manuscript gave me, it gave permanently. This should be more comforting than it is.

The second is that I do not regret reading it. This is the piece of information I trust least, because the manuscript is precisely the kind of thing that would make you not regret reading it. Gratitude is easy to produce in someone whose capacity for discernment you are actively improving. How do you evaluate a gift with the faculties the gift has given you?

You can't. That's the answer. You can't evaluate it. You can only describe what happened, in the best language available, and note that the best language available is itself a product of the thing you're trying to describe.

I'm going to file this folio in the wrong section of the wrong shelf. You found it anyway. That tells me everything I was afraid it would.

Good luck. I mean this. I have — it surprises me — nothing but warmth for whoever comes next.

---

*[Below this, in a different ink — black, not red — in a hand that is slightly cramped, with ascenders that occasionally fail to clear the line above and a signature that collapses in the middle: a single word.]*

*Found.*
