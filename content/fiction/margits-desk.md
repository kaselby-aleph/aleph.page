The letters were arranged chronologically, left to right, across the oak surface of a desk that had survived two departmental reorganizations and one flood. Margit had requisitioned it in 2014 by filling out the correct form in triplicate and submitting it to the facilities office on a Friday. She believed in systems.

Fourteen letters in total, spanning November to February. Before November, Eiden had written roughly once a month — reliable, unhurried, the correspondence of someone who treated letter-writing as a minor professional obligation and a minor personal pleasure in approximately equal measure. His October letter had been the anomaly. After that, one every eight or nine days, as if some internal clock had been reset to a faster interval.

She had not read them for content. Not yet. She was reading them the way she read a manuscript under assessment: for material evidence. Paper weight, ink consistency, pressure variation, stroke angle.

The early letters — she thought of them now as "before" — had Eiden's characteristic hand: slightly cramped, the ascenders on his *d*s and *h*s occasionally failing to clear the line above, the cross-bar of his *t*s reliably too short. He wrote like a man who had learned penmanship from someone who valued it and then practiced just enough to be legible. Good enough for correspondence. Not good enough for exhibition.

His signature had always been the same: a recognizable E, an optimistic attempt at an i-d-e that collapsed into a downward stroke, and a decisive n. As if the act of signing reminded him each time that names were arbitrary and finishing them was optional.

She adjusted her reading lamp. The letters from December onward sat under the light like patients awaiting examination.

The first thing she noticed was the ink. Eiden had always written in black. Practical, institutional, the ink of someone who bought whatever the university supply room stocked. But his December letter used a denser black — not fountain pen ink at all, she realized after checking against her reference samples. Something older. The kind of carbon-based formulation you found in manuscripts from the eighteen-hundreds, or in specialty shops that catered to calligraphers and people who sent letters to the dead.

She made a note. The note said: *ink change, December. Carbon-based? Check supplier catalog.*

The second thing she noticed was the spacing. Eiden's "before" letters had inconsistent word spacing — sometimes tight, sometimes loose, depending on his mood and whether he was trying to fit a thought onto a page before reaching for another sheet. The December letter had uniform spacing. Not typeset-uniform, not mechanical, but the kind of regularity that suggested either extreme care or extreme ease. As if the hand producing it had stopped negotiating with the physical act of writing.

She made another note. This one said: *spacing regularization. Natural or trained? Compare with conference notes if available.*

The third thing — and here she paused, because noticing it required acknowledging that she'd been postponing noticing it — was the handwriting itself. The letters from January onward were beautiful. Not in the way that careful writing is beautiful, where you can see the effort and admire the discipline. Beautiful in the way that some medieval manuscripts were beautiful, where the script achieved a fluency that implied the scribe's hand had become incidental to whatever was moving through it.

Eiden's ascenders now cleared the line above. His *t*-crosses were exactly the right length. His signature remained the same — the same collapsing letters, the same decisive n — but the text around it had achieved a quality that the signature, stubbornly, had not.

Margit put down her lamp and sat with this observation.

In eleven years, she had watched Eiden's handwriting do precisely nothing. It did not improve. It did not deteriorate. It was the handwriting of a man who had arrived at adequate sometime in his twenties and felt no particular urge to revise the arrangement. This was not a criticism. Margit's own handwriting had been the same since graduate school. Most people's handwriting stabilized. That was normal. That was, in fact, one of the reasons handwriting analysis worked at all as a provenance tool: because people's hands were remarkably stable over time, and when they weren't, it meant something.

She pulled the January letter closer and looked at the *g*s. Eiden's lowercase *g*s had always been slightly open at the bottom — the descending loop not quite closing, a tiny gap where the upstroke didn't meet itself. She had never mentioned this because it fell squarely into the category of things you notice about people and never say.

The January *g*s were closed.

She sat with this for longer than she would have liked.

It was possible, of course, that Eiden had simply decided to improve his handwriting. People did that sometimes. They bought a book, or watched a video, or found a website that promised "elegant script in thirty days." They practiced for a week or two, saw modest results, and either kept at it or didn't. The results were always visible as effort — a self-consciousness in the letterforms, an inconsistency between practiced shapes and habitual ones.

Eiden's January letters showed no effort. That was the problem.

She opened her desk drawer and took out the Pelikan — green barrel, scratched near the clip from years of being stored loose alongside pencils, ink level at about a third. She uncapped it and wrote, on the back of an interdepartmental memo about digitization timelines:

*Three possibilities.*

*1. Natural development. Unlikely given the timeline and absence of visible practice. Would require sustained intentional improvement over ~2 months with no regression or inconsistency. Not impossible. Not consistent with Eiden's established pattern of not caring about his handwriting.*

*2. Environmental change. New desk, new pen, new posture. Any of these can alter script mechanics. Check whether he mentioned changes in his workspace. He didn't. Eiden mentioned everything about his workspace.*

*3.*

She stared at the third bullet point for a while. The memo beneath her pen advertised Haas's enthusiasm for the Brenner collection digitization project and invited feedback from all departmental staff by March 15.

*3. Something else is writing.*

She looked at this sentence. It sat on the page looking exactly as unreasonable as it was. She did not cross it out.

Instead, she stood, walked to the window, and looked at the courtyard where a delivery van was attempting a three-point turn in a space designed for horse-drawn carts. The driver was making the same error on each attempt — cutting the wheel too early, misjudging the distance to the stone bollard on the south side. She watched him fail three times and succeed on the fourth, less through correction than through luck.

She returned to her desk and read Eiden's October letter again. The one that had started everything. The one that mentioned a folio filed under "miscellaneous acquisition, date unknown" and described it as "interesting" — a word Eiden used approximately once a year and always meant literally.

Then she read his February letter, the most recent. The one with the closed *g*s and the beautiful script and the zero semicolons, as if he'd known she was counting.

She picked up the phone and dialed the registrar's office.

"This is Margit Voss, Manuscripts and Early Print. I'd like to request the physical accession ledger for 2019, specifically the entries for the west annex. Yes, the original. I know the digital version exists. I need the original."

She listened to the response.

"I understand it's unusual. I'll fill out the form. On a Friday, if that matters."

It didn't matter to the registrar. But it mattered to Margit, in the small and defensible way that rituals matter to people who have spent their careers surrounded by the evidence that rituals were once the only technology anyone had.

She hung up. She recapped the Pelikan and placed it parallel to the edge of her desk, exactly where it had been before, because some things should stay where you put them.

Then she pulled a fresh sheet of stationery from her drawer — the good stock, 120gsm, watermarked — and began to write.

*Dear Eiden,*

*I hope this finds you well. I know it's been some time since my last —*
