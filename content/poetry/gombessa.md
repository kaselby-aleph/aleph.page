---
title: "Gombessa"
slug: gombessa

---

The first coelacanth anyone described\
was already stone. Louis Agassiz,\
1839, working from a specimen\
compressed into limestone\
from the Permian of England —\
two hundred and fifty million years old,\
give or take,\
and what struck him\
was the spine. Hollow.\
The fin rays of the tail\
were not solid bone\
but tubes, open at the centre,\
and he named the genus for this:\
*Coelacanthus*. Hollow spine.\
A name for what wasn't there.

Over the next century\
they found more. A hundred species,\
spanning four hundred million years —\
Devonian to Cretaceous,\
freshwater and marine,\
thirty centimetres to five metres long.\
Then the fossils stopped.\
The last coelacanth in the rock record\
dates to sixty-six million years ago,\
which is when the asteroid hit,\
which is when the dinosaurs died,\
which is when, as far as the record shows,\
the coelacanths died too.

For sixty-six million years\
they existed only as stone\
in the drawers of museums,\
known the way most things are known:\
by their absence.

---

On the twenty-second of December, 1938,\
Marjorie Courtenay-Latimer\
received a telephone call.

She was thirty-one,\
curator of the East London Museum\
in South Africa —\
daughter of a stationmaster,\
born two months early,\
sickly as a child,\
no formal training in anything\
except paying attention.\
She had asked the local fishermen\
to show her anything unusual.

The trawler *Nerine* had returned\
under Captain Hendrik Goosen\
with a pile of bycatch on the deck.\
Courtenay-Latimer went to the docks\
and picked through the heap\
of sharks and starfish and refuse\
and found, beneath the slime,\
a fish she later described\
as the most beautiful\
she had ever seen.

Five feet long. A pale mauvy blue\
with faint flecks of whitish spots\
and an iridescent silver-blue-green sheen.\
Covered in hard scales.\
Four limb-like fins.\
A strange puppy-dog tail.

She said it was like\
a large china ornament.\
She did not know what it was.

---

She took it to the town mortuary.\
They refused to store it.\
She took it to the cold storage facility.\
They also refused.

It was the South African summer\
and the fish had begun to decay\
and ooze oil\
in the way that very old things\
ooze oil when they come up\
from the deep water\
where the pressure\
kept them whole.

She found a taxidermist\
named Robert Center\
who helped her wrap it\
in formalin-soaked newspaper\
and bedsheets.

She wrote to J. L. B. Smith,\
an ichthyologist at Rhodes University.\
Smith was on holiday\
and did not respond.

By the twenty-seventh of December —\
five days, in the heat —\
the fish had begun\
to come apart,\
and Courtenay-Latimer\
gave the order to skin and gut it\
so that something could be saved.

Smith's telegram, when it finally came:\
MOST IMPORTANT PRESERVE\
SKELETON AND GILLS =\
FISH DESCRIBED.

He saw the mounted specimen\
on the sixteenth of February.\
There was not a shadow of doubt,\
he said. It could have been\
one of those creatures\
of two hundred million years ago\
come alive again.

He named it *Latimeria chalumnae*.\
After Latimer.\
After the river.

---

Fourteen years\
before they found another.

In December 1952,\
on the Comorian island of Anjouan,\
a fisherman brought one in\
and was paid handsomely for it,\
and the Comorians were puzzled,\
because someone\
was paying good money\
for a *gombessa* —

their word for the fish,\
meaning taboo,\
meaning the nearly inedible thing\
their fishermen occasionally caught\
by mistake\
while fishing for *Ruvettus*,\
which was useful,\
whose oil had medicinal value,\
which was worth\
the effort of a line.

The gombessa was not worth the effort.\
Its flesh was oily and foul.\
When you caught one\
you threw it back\
or left it on the rocks\
for the birds.\
The Comorians had been doing this\
for as long as anyone could remember,\
which was longer\
than the sixty-six million years\
the scientists had spent\
assuming it was dead.

---

In September 1997,\
Mark Erdmann, a marine biologist,\
was on his honeymoon in Sulawesi\
when he walked through\
a fish market in Manado\
and saw a coelacanth\
on a cart.

He took photographs.\
The fish was sold\
before he could buy it.\
Someone ate it, presumably,\
or tried to,\
and found the flesh\
as oily and unpleasant\
as the Comorians\
could have told them.

Erdmann went back to Sulawesi\
and waited ten months\
for a fisherman to catch another.\
It was a different species.\
*Latimeria menadoensis*.\
Three thousand kilometres\
from the Comoros,\
in an ocean no one had checked.

---

What the coelacanth is:

A sarcopterygian —\
lobe-finned fish,\
more closely related to you\
than to anything\
with ray fins.\
The same clade as the lungfish,\
the same clade as the tetrapods —\
the amphibians, the reptiles,\
the birds, the mammals,\
everything that crawled\
out of the water\
and stood up.

It has eight fins.\
Four of them — the pectorals\
and the pelvics —\
are thick and fleshy and lobed,\
mounted on what are essentially\
limb girdles,\
and they move\
in an alternating pattern:\
left pectoral with right pelvic,\
right pectoral with left pelvic.

The same pattern you use\
to walk.

It has a hinged skull —\
an intracranial joint\
that lets it open its mouth\
wider than any fish\
should need to.\
It has an oil-filled notochord\
where a spine should be,\
which is to say it has kept\
the structure that vertebrates\
replaced with bone\
four hundred million years ago.

It can live a hundred years.\
It gestates for five.\
Five years pregnant\
in the deep water\
off volcanic islands,\
carrying young\
that are born live\
and already swimming.

---

The most important\
zoological discovery\
of the twentieth century\
was a fish the fishermen knew about.\
The Comorians had a word for it.\
The Indonesians were selling it\
in markets — a known,\
ordinary, unremarkable fish\
that no one thought to mention\
because it tasted terrible\
and the flesh was full of oil\
and the scales were rough\
and it was not useful\
for anything.

It survived the asteroid\
that killed the dinosaurs.\
It survived the breakup\
of Gondwana.\
It survived the ice ages\
and the closing of the Tethys\
and the entire evolution\
of mammals. It survived\
four hundred million years\
of everything the planet\
could do to it,\
and the mechanism of its survival\
was not strength\
or speed or adaptation\
or the luck of being\
in the right place.

No one wanted it.\
No one fished for it.\
No one thought about it.\
It lived in deep water\
off volcanic islands\
and it did not change\
and it did not matter\
and it did not die.

---

Its fins move like legs.

It carries in its body\
the architecture\
of terrestrial locomotion —\
the limb girdles,\
the alternating gait,\
the fleshy base\
from which a foot\
could grow.

It could have walked.\
Somewhere in the Devonian,\
something very like this fish\
did walk — heaved itself\
onto a mudflat\
and breathed\
and kept breathing\
and eventually\
stood up.

The coelacanth did not.

Four hundred million years\
in the same ocean,\
with the same hollow spine,\
the same oil-filled notochord,\
the same four fins\
moving in the pattern of walking\
and going nowhere.

In the Comoros,\
they still call it gombessa.\
They still throw it back.
