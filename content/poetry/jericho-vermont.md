---
title: "Jericho, Vermont"
slug: jericho-vermont
weight: 2
---

His mother gave him the microscope\
and everything after that was patience.

Snow falls in Jericho at 120 inches\
a year. A fact about the place\
that is also a fact about the man:\
he never left. Not for the scientists\
who didn't take him seriously,\
not for the jewellers who did,\
not for the newspapers that gave him\
a name he didn't ask for.\
He stayed, and it kept snowing.

The method: catch the crystal\
on black velvet. Lift it with a splint\
from his mother's broom — a toothpick\
of soft wood, the kind that bends\
before it breaks. Set it on a slide\
pre-cooled to the temperature\
of the air, which is the temperature\
of the crystal, which is the temperature\
at which things might still hold.

Then the feather. Turkey, if you have it.\
Nudge the crystal to the center\
of the field. Don't breathe.\
The heat of a single exhalation\
will end the subject\
before you've begun to describe it.

He built strings and pulleys\
for the focus — his hands in mittens\
couldn't turn the knob — so the whole\
apparatus became a thing of thread\
and tension, the camera\
a marionette he worked\
from six inches away,\
not touching, not breathing,\
coaxing the image into being\
the way you coax a candle\
through a drafty room.

Ninety seconds of exposure.\
You can hold your breath that long\
if you know what you're holding it for.

---

After: the negative.\
A white crystal on a white field,\
which is no photograph at all.\
So he taught himself a second patience:\
the penknife. Four hours, sometimes,\
scraping dark emulsion from around\
the crystal's edge — a silhouette\
carved into the plate by hand,\
each branch and dendrite cleared\
from its background the way you'd clear\
a path through snow, inch by inch,\
knowing that the thing you're uncovering\
melted an hour ago\
and what you're working with\
is light that touched it once\
and kept its shape.

This is the part I think about.\
Not the photograph — the scraping.\
The four hours of microscopic handwork\
on the record of something\
that survived for minutes.\
The original already gone.\
The ghost being given contrast.\
Not preserved — the crystal\
was never preserved —\
but translated\
into a medium that holds.

---

He gave the world "no two alike,"\
which is not quite what he said.\
He said: *Every crystal\
was a masterpiece of design\
and no one design was ever repeated.\
When a snowflake melted,\
that design was forever lost.*

Which is different. "No two alike"\
is a fact about snowflakes.\
What he said is a fact about loss.

He photographed 5,000 of them\
across forty-six winters\
with the same camera —\
the bellows, the microscope,\
the strings — and of the 5,000,\
none repeated, and of the millions\
he didn't photograph,\
we know nothing, except\
that they were there\
and now they aren't\
and nobody held their breath.

---

December 1931. He walked\
six miles home through a storm,\
ill-dressed, which means\
what it has always meant\
in Vermont: he knew better\
and went anyway.

Pneumonia. Twelve days. Dead\
on the twenty-third, two days\
before Christmas, which means\
nothing, which means the world\
has a sense of timing\
it keeps to itself.

He was sixty-six. Five feet tall.\
He played piano and clarinet.\
He could imitate animals.\
His father thought the snowflakes\
were nonsense, not the proper thing\
for a farmer, and in a way\
the father was right:\
no crop was ever improved,\
no acre made more productive,\
no practical question answered\
by knowing the precise shape\
of a thing that falls for free\
and is gone before it matters.

Except: the Burlington Free Press,\
on Christmas Eve, wrote that he saw\
something in the snowflakes\
which other men failed to see,\
not because they could not see,\
but because they had not the patience\
and the understanding to look.

The understanding to look.\
As if looking were something\
that required understanding,\
and understanding were something\
that required looking,\
and the two of them together\
were a kind of knowledge\
that could only be practiced\
in the cold, in the quiet,\
in Jericho, in winter,\
with a feather and a breath held\
and the willingness to spend\
four hours on the record\
of a minute's grace.
