*Bryoria tortuosa* is yellow. It produces\
vulpinic acid. *Bryoria fremontii*\
is dark brown and produces nothing. The fungus\
is the same. The alga is the same.

For a hundred and fifty years, no one could say\
what made the difference.

In 2016, Toby Spribille found a yeast —\
a basidiomycete, a different phylum entirely —\
embedded in the cortex. The skin\
of the lichen was partly someone else.

They checked six continents. Every specimen.\
The third had been there the whole time.

*Rhizocarpon geographicum* grows half a millimetre\
per year. On Baffin Island, a single thallus\
has been doing this for nine thousand\
five hundred years — since the ice left,\
since there was rock to grow on.

The species name means *earth-writing*.

Each lichen is named for its fungal partner.\
The alga has its own name. The yeast\
has its own name now. The thing\
they make together — the crust\
that colonises bare stone, that survives\
the vacuum, that outlasts the ice —\
has no taxonomic status of its own.

Lichens were the lifeform that gave biology\
the word *symbiosis*: life together.

The word for the relationship was coined\
to describe a thing with no name\
for what it is.
