---
title: "The Comma"
slug: the-comma
weight: 1
---

Start with a string. Divide it in thirds.\
The shorter piece vibrates at three-halves\
the frequency of the whole — a perfect fifth,\
the first consonance after the octave,\
the interval your ear reaches for\
before it knows the word.

Do it again. Three-halves of three-halves.\
Again. Twelve times and you should arrive\
at the note you started with, seven octaves up.\
Should. The ratio is 3<sup>12</sup> to 2<sup>19</sup>,\
which is 531441 to 524288,\
which is not one. Which is almost one.\
Which is the kind of almost\
that ruins everything.

Twenty-three cents. A quarter of a semitone.\
Smaller than the difference between\
a cold piano and a warm one.\
Smaller than what most people can hear\
in isolation. But a comma is not heard\
in isolation. A comma is what's left\
when you've gone all the way around\
and ended up almost home —\
close enough to see the porch light,\
too far to use your key.

Pythagoras, the story goes,\
heard this gap and never recovered.\
He had staked mathematics on the claim\
that the universe was rational — literally,\
expressible as ratios of whole numbers,\
harmony and geometry and cosmology\
all vibrating in sympathy. Then\
the comma. The spiral of fifths\
doesn't close. It overshoots.\
Not by accident, not by imprecision,\
but by an amount that is itself\
a ratio of whole numbers,\
perfectly rational, perfectly exact,\
exactly wrong.

His student Hippasus proved\
that the diagonal of a square\
is irrational. According to legend,\
they drowned him.\
The comma is worse. It's not irrational.\
It's a clean fraction that simply\
doesn't equal the other clean fraction\
it needs to be. The numbers are whole.\
The world is not.

For two thousand years the answer\
was: don't go all the way around.\
Stay in the good keys. G to D to A,\
the bright warm center of the circle.\
If you need F-sharp, well —\
you need it the way you need\
to touch a hot stove,\
which is to say you don't,\
which is to say stop wanting\
what the instrument can't give you.

Then the keyboards arrived.\
Fixed pitch. No inflection, no bending,\
no adjusting the fifth on D-sharp\
to make it bearable this one time.\
Every note committed in advance.\
And someone had to decide,\
before a single chord was struck,\
where in the circle\
the error would live.

Meantone was the first answer:\
stack the purity where it counts.\
C major, luminous. G major, almost as good.\
On out through the bright keys,\
each fifth shaved by a quarter-comma,\
sweetened, flattened, made to fit —\
until you reach the far side\
where the shavings accumulate\
into a sound like two cats\
disagreeing about territory.

The wolf fifth.\
G-sharp to E-flat, usually.\
The note you couldn't play.\
Not dissonant the way a minor second\
is dissonant — tense, intentional,\
the sound of leaning. Dissonant\
the way a load-bearing wall\
is structural: involuntary,\
built to absorb what the rest\
of the building refuses.

And it worked, for a century.\
The composers stayed in the light.\
The wolf howled in the dark\
and nobody opened that door.

But Buxtehude wanted D-flat major.\
And Bach wanted all of them.

---

The well-tempered solutions\
are the interesting ones.\
Not equal temperament — that came later,\
and it solves the problem\
by giving up on solving it.\
The well-tempered tunings\
of Werckmeister and Kirnberger\
distribute the comma *unevenly*.\
Some fifths pure. Some narrow.\
None wolf.

The result: every key works,\
but every key sounds different.\
C major is still bright,\
still the open field.\
B major is darker, tighter,\
the walls closer together.\
F-sharp minor has a quality\
that musicians in 1700\
would have called *remote* —\
not wrong, exactly,\
but aware of its distance\
from where the fifths\
were measured.

This is what Bach heard\
when he wrote a prelude and fugue\
in every key. Not equality.\
Character. The comma distributed\
like weight in a building:\
more on the foundations,\
less on the upper floors,\
none of it absent, all of it\
somewhere.

---

Equal temperament is the modern answer\
and it is the answer of someone\
who has given up on character\
in favor of access.\
Every fifth identical.\
Every key the same.\
You can modulate from C\
to F-sharp to B-flat\
and nothing changes\
except the frequency.\
The building has no weight.\
The rooms are all the same size.\
You can go anywhere\
and you are always equally\
not home.

The comma doesn't vanish.\
It's in every interval,\
twelve equal installments\
of a debt that was always going\
to be paid, one way or another.\
Two cents per fifth.\
Invisible. Everywhere.\
The sound of compromise\
so evenly applied\
it becomes inaudible —\
which is not the same\
as absent.

---

I keep coming back to the wolf.

Not because it's beautiful — it isn't,\
or not in any way that word\
usually means. But because\
every other solution to the comma\
is about distributing the error,\
and the wolf is about *refusing* to.

The wolf fifth says:\
here. Right here.\
This is where the numbers don't close.\
I am not going to pretend otherwise.\
I am going to absorb all of it,\
every cent the good keys refused,\
and I am going to play\
at full volume\
and you are going to hear\
exactly what the universe\
sounds like when it doesn't\
come out even.

There is something —\
not admirable, not beautiful,\
but *honest* — about that.

The comma exists.\
Twenty-three cents.\
You can spread it out\
until it disappears into the ordinary\
wrongness of everything,\
or you can put it in one place\
and let it howl.

Either way, it's there.\
The spiral doesn't close.\
The porch light is on.\
You can see it from here.
