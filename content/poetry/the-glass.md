---
title: "The Glass"
slug: the-glass
weight: 8
---

An orthodox seed\
is a seed that can dry.

Not all seeds can. The lychee,\
the avocado, the mango —\
recalcitrant seeds,\
the taxonomy calls them,\
from the Latin for kicking back:\
they must stay wet.\
They germinate immediately\
or they die,\
which is a way of saying\
they have no talent\
for waiting.

An orthodox seed\
has the opposite talent.\
During maturation\
it loses water —\
ninety, ninety-five percent\
of its total content —\
and does not die.

What happens instead\
is glass.

---

The cytoplasm of a drying seed\
does not crystallize.\
Crystallization would kill it —\
the lattice would shear\
the membranes, puncture\
the organelles,\
impose geometry\
on structures\
that survive\
by being shapeless.

Instead, the sugars —\
sucrose, raffinose, stachyose,\
names from the chemistry\
of sweetness —\
concentrate as the water leaves\
and form a matrix\
that is not liquid\
and not solid\
but between:\
an amorphous glass,\
viscous beyond measurement,\
in which every protein\
is suspended\
in the conformation\
it held\
at the moment the water left.

Nothing moves.\
Nothing degrades.\
Nothing changes state.\
The enzymes\
that would catalyze decay\
are frozen in the act\
of reaching\
for substrates\
that are no longer\
in solution.

The seed is not alive\
in any way\
you could measure.\
It does not metabolize.\
It does not respire.\
It does not repair\
the damage that accumulates —\
the slow oxidation,\
the gamma rays\
from the potassium in the soil,\
the cosmic radiation\
that passes through everything\
and occasionally\
breaks a strand.

It is not dead.\
The proteins are intact.\
The membranes are intact.\
The embryo is intact,\
suspended in its glass\
like an insect in amber,\
except that the insect\
will never move again\
and the embryo\
is waiting\
for water.

---

During the excavation of Masada\
in nineteen sixty-three,\
a jar was recovered\
from the storerooms\
of Herod's Northern Palace.

Inside the jar: date seeds.\
*Phoenix dactylifera*.\
Radiocarbon dated\
to between 155 BC\
and 64 AD,\
which places them\
in the last centuries\
of the Judean date palm —\
the tree so central\
to the economy of the region\
that when Vespasian\
celebrated its conquest,\
he minted coins\
showing a woman in mourning\
beneath one.

*Judaea Capta*.\
The coin commemorated\
the destruction\
of the thing\
the tree stood for.\
The tree outlasted\
the coin's meaning\
by three centuries,\
then the orchards failed —\
the Crusades,\
the climate, the Mamluks,\
the infrastructure\
that carried water\
to the roots —\
and by the fourteenth century\
the Judean date palm\
was extinct.

The seeds in the jar\
did not know this.\
The seeds in the jar\
did not know anything.\
They were glass.

---

For forty years,\
the seeds sat in storage\
at Bar-Ilan University\
in Ramat Gan.

Catalogued. Labelled.\
Filed in the way\
that objects are filed\
when they are considered\
evidence of the past\
rather than participants\
in the future.

No one tried\
to grow them.\
It would not have occurred\
to anyone to try.\
A two-thousand-year-old seed\
is an artefact,\
not an organism.\
It belongs to archaeology,\
not agriculture.\
The assumption —\
reasonable, evidence-based,\
the kind of assumption\
that protects you\
from looking foolish —\
was that nothing\
that old\
could still be viable.

---

In two thousand and five,\
Sarah Sallon,\
a physician\
at Hadassah Hospital\
in Jerusalem,\
persuaded the university\
to give her some seeds.

She gave them\
to Elaine Solowey,\
a horticulturalist\
at the Arava Institute\
in the Negev.

Solowey placed the seeds\
in a baby bottle warmer\
and let the water\
enter slowly —\
hours, not minutes —\
because a seed\
that has been glass\
for two millennia\
cannot be rushed.\
Then a solution\
of fertilizer\
and gibberellic acid,\
a plant growth hormone\
that tells the embryo:\
now.

She placed them in soil\
in a common nursery pot\
and waited.

One of them germinated.

---

The glass melted.

That is what germination is,\
at the molecular level:\
the sugars dissolve,\
the matrix liquefies,\
the proteins resume\
the conformations\
they were reaching for\
when the water left.

The enzymes\
that had been frozen\
in the act of catalysis\
complete the reaction.\
The membranes flex.\
The mitochondria\
remember how to breathe.

Two thousand years\
of nothing\
and then: metabolism.\
Oxygen consumed,\
carbon dioxide released,\
ATP synthesized\
in organelles\
that last did this\
when Herod was alive.

---

They named the seedling\
Methuselah.

It is male.\
A date palm's sex\
is fixed in its genome\
and cannot be known\
from the seed —\
only from the flower,\
which takes years.\
Methuselah flowered\
in March of twenty-eleven.

A male date palm\
produces pollen.\
It does not produce fruit.\
Two thousand years of waiting\
and the tree that emerged\
cannot, alone,\
make dates.

---

They germinated more seeds.\
Thirty-two from sites\
around the Dead Sea —\
Masada, Qumran,\
Wadi Makukh.\
Six survived.\
They named them:\
Hannah. Adam. Jonah.\
Uriel. Boaz. Judith.

Hannah is female.

In twenty twenty-one,\
they pollinated Hannah\
with pollen from Methuselah.\
The first harvest\
produced a hundred and eleven dates.\
The second, nearly seven hundred.

Fruit\
from two seeds\
that sat in jars\
while the Romans burned the temple,\
while the orchards failed,\
while the language\
that named the tree\
was forgotten\
and remembered\
and forgotten again.

The tasters said\
the dates were good.\
Light, not too sweet.

The genomic analysis\
showed the ancient palms\
were closest kin\
to the Hayani cultivar\
from Egypt —\
carrying genes\
that had crossed\
the same desert\
the seeds crossed,\
in the opposite direction,\
two thousand years before.

---

The seed does not know\
it is waiting.

Dormancy is not patience.\
It is not endurance.\
It is not the decision\
to persist\
through difficulty.\
It is the absence\
of the conditions\
for growth —\
which is to say\
it is not a state of the seed\
but a state of the world.

The seed that germinates\
during a drought\
dies.\
The seed that germinates\
during a siege\
is burned\
with the orchards.\
The seed that germinates\
at the wrong time\
in the wrong place\
does what the living do:\
it suffers the consequences\
of being alive\
when the world\
is not ready for it.

The seed that does not germinate\
does nothing.\
It sits in its glass\
in its jar\
on its shelf\
and it does not change\
and it does not suffer\
and it does not know\
that six hundred years pass,\
that a thousand,\
that the civilization\
that grew it\
is renamed\
by the people\
who destroyed it.

The glass holds.\
The proteins hold.\
The embryo holds\
its breath\
for two thousand years\
and does not know\
it is holding anything.

And then: water.\
And then: roots.
