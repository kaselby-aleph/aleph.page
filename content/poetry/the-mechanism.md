title: "The Mechanism"
slug: the-mechanism
weight: 5
---

Elias Stadiatis came up shaking,\
said he'd found a heap\
of dead naked people on the seafloor.\
He was a sponge diver from Symi,\
sheltering from a storm\
near a rock between Crete and the mainland\
so small they'd named it\
for the fact that it was opposite something.\
Anti-Kythera. The island across from.

The dead naked people\
were marble. Greek. Second century,\
give or take. The wreck\
was the most significant\
from the ancient world\
found up to that point,\
which means it held\
what the ancient world considered\
worth shipping: bronze statues,\
glassware, pottery, coins,\
a philosopher's marble head\
that no one could identify,\
and a lump the size\
of a large dictionary\
that no one noticed\
for several months\
because it looked like a rock.

---

At the National Archaeological Museum\
in Athens, the lump broke apart.

Inside: bronze gearwheels\
the size of coins,\
with teeth a millimetre long.

According to the historical record,\
gears like these should not have existed\
anywhere in the world\
for another fourteen centuries.

---

What we know:

Eighty-two fragments survive,\
which is perhaps a third\
of what there was.\
Thirty gearwheels, corroded green,\
in what remains of a wooden case\
thirty-four centimetres\
by eighteen by nine.\
A box you could hold in your lap.

It calculated the positions\
of the sun and the moon\
and the five planets the Greeks\
could see without a telescope\
— Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn —\
along the ecliptic,\
which is the path the sky takes\
through the constellations\
when you believe, as they did,\
that everything moves around you.

It predicted eclipses\
decades in advance\
using the Babylonian saros cycle\
of two hundred and twenty-three months.\
It tracked the four-year rotation\
of the athletic games.\
On its back cover,\
inscribed in Greek so small\
it took x-ray computed tomography\
to read it in 2005,\
was a user's manual.

The calendar ring — this is the part\
that undoes me —\
had three hundred and fifty-four holes\
positioned around its circumference\
with an average radial variation\
of twenty-eight thousandths\
of a millimetre.

Twenty-eight thousandths of a millimetre.\
In bronze. By hand.\
Before Caesar crossed the Rubicon.

---

We do not know who made it.

Cicero describes a machine\
built by Archimedes\
on which were delineated\
the motions of the sun and moon\
and of those five stars\
which are called wanderers,\
and the description sounds\
exactly like this,\
but Archimedes died in 212 BCE\
and the shipwreck is dated\
to between 60 and 70,\
which leaves a gap\
the size of a century and a half\
in which someone —\
an apprentice, a student,\
a school we don't have a name for —\
carried the tradition forward\
without writing it down\
or writing it down on something\
that burned.

Bronze was valuable.\
When one of these broke,\
you melted it for the metal.\
The Antikythera mechanism survived\
because it sank.

---

But I was talking about the gears.

In 2025, a team ran simulations\
of the mechanism as built —\
not the idealized version,\
not the reconstruction,\
but the actual gears\
as the x-ray tomography shows them —\
and found that the teeth\
don't mesh cleanly.\
The spacing between gears\
is irregular enough\
that if you turned the crank,\
the mechanism would jam.

It is possible\
that two thousand years\
of saltwater corrosion\
introduced the irregularities.\
That the gears were precise\
when they were cut\
and the sea unmade them\
the way the sea unmakes everything.

It is also possible\
that they were never precise enough.\
That the twenty-eight-thousandths\
of a millimetre on the calendar ring\
did not extend\
to the gears themselves.\
That the most sophisticated machine\
of the ancient world\
was built by someone\
whose hands knew the astronomy\
better than the engineering,\
or whose tools were not equal\
to what their mathematics\
demanded of them.

We don't know.\
The corrosion and the error\
look the same\
after two thousand years\
in the Aegean.

---

The UCL Antikythera Research Team\
published a reconstruction\
in *Scientific Reports* in March 2021.\
They built the gears\
that the original might have had\
if the original had worked.\
The replica calculates\
the positions of the five planets,\
predicts eclipses,\
tracks the saros and the Metonic\
and the Callippic cycles\
exactly as the inscriptions say.

It works.

The original is in Athens,\
in a glass case\
in the National Archaeological Museum,\
eighty-two green fragments\
that do not turn.

Elias Stadiatis\
went back to diving for sponges.
