title: "The Method"
slug: the-method

---

Start with the goat.

Before it was a page it was an animal\
on a hillside in what is now Turkey\
and was then the eastern edge\
of an empire that copied everything\
and forgot to say who did the copying.\
The hide was soaked in lime\
for a week, maybe longer,\
until the hair released —\
then scraped with a lunellum,\
a blade shaped like a crescent moon\
and designed for this:\
the removal of everything\
that made the surface alive\
until what remains is collagen,\
which is structure,\
which is the part of the animal\
that holds the rest of the animal\
together, and which,\
once the rest is gone,\
will hold whatever\
someone puts there next.

Stretched on a frame.\
Dried in the air.\
Cut into sheets, folded into quires,\
and a scribe — Constantinople,\
tenth century, no name —\
sat down with a reed\
and copied Archimedes.

---

The ink was iron gall.\
Oak galls and iron sulfate,\
gum arabic, something acidic —\
vinegar, wine, the recipes vary\
by workshop and by century\
and by what was on the shelf\
the morning the scribe mixed a batch.\
The chemistry is simple.\
The tannin in the gall\
reacts with the iron\
and the product — ferric tannate,\
blue-black, permanent — bonds\
to the collagen of the skin.\
Not onto the skin. *Into* it.\
The iron migrates\
past the surface, locks itself\
to the protein fibres\
at a depth that matters.

Remember this.\
No one else does.\
Not for eight hundred years.

What the scribe was copying:\
seven treatises by Archimedes,\
including one he'd sent\
as a letter to Eratosthenes\
in Alexandria. He called it\
*The Method of Mechanical Theorems.*

In it, he describes a way\
of weighing shapes.\
Slice a parabola\
into infinitely thin planes.\
Place them on a lever.\
Balance them against planes\
cut from a shape\
whose area you already know.\
If the lever balances,\
the areas are equal.

This is integral calculus.\
He found it in Syracuse\
in the third century before Christ,\
two thousand years before\
Newton and Leibniz\
reinvented it in the same decade\
and spent the rest of their lives\
arguing about who deserved the credit.\
Archimedes did not call it calculus.\
He didn't trust it enough\
to call it a theorem.\
He called it a method —\
a way of seeing, not a proof,\
a sketch of what the argument\
would look like when someone found\
the right formalism,\
which nobody did,\
not for two millennia,\
partly because this letter\
was about to be scraped off\
a goatskin and replaced\
with the evening liturgy.

---

April 1229.\
We know the date because the monk\
who did it — Johannes Myronas —\
signed the colophon\
of the book he made\
from the parchment\
of the book he unmade.

The procedure:\
unbind the quires.\
Soak the parchment.\
Scrape the ink with pumice stone —\
some say a blade,\
the sources don't agree,\
and the parchment has declined\
to comment.\
The text lifts off.\
Most of it.\
The iron in the ink,\
which locked itself to the collagen\
at a depth the pumice\
couldn't reach,\
stays.\
But it fades. It ghosts.\
It becomes invisible\
to anyone who isn't looking,\
and no one is looking,\
because what Myronas does next\
is the thing that interests me.

He rotates the pages\
ninety degrees.

He takes the scraped sheets\
and refolds them sideways —\
new quires, new binding,\
the old text now running\
perpendicular to the new.\
And he writes his prayers\
left to right across a surface\
where the mathematics reads\
top to bottom, and if you open\
the finished book in 1230\
you see an ordinary euchologion,\
prayers for the evening service,\
and the ghost of Archimedes\
runs crosswise through the liturgy\
like a current under ice —\
present, invisible,\
reading in a direction\
no one thought to look.

---

He was not wrong to do this.

A monk in a monastery\
needs liturgical texts.\
He does not need a technique\
for computing the area\
under a parabola.\
The parchment was expensive.\
The mathematics was,\
to a thirteenth-century monk,\
a dead language about a dead subject\
by a dead man in a dead city,\
and the Greek he could read\
was the Greek of the psalms,\
not the Greek of mechanical theorems.\
There is nothing villainous\
about turning the things you can't use\
into the things you can.

This is what every century does\
to the century before it:\
keeps what it needs,\
scrapes what it doesn't,\
writes its own text\
over the remainder,\
reading in its own direction.

But the prayer book survived.\
I want to say this twice,\
because it sounds like loss\
and it isn't.\
The prayer book *survived.*

Across the Ottoman conquest,\
across fires and floods\
and the long, slow catastrophe\
of institutional neglect\
that is the ordinary biography\
of any book in any century.\
The prayer book survived\
because a prayer book is used.\
You open it on Sundays.\
You read from it,\
and because you read from it\
you repair it, and because\
you repair it you keep it,\
and the liturgical calendar\
is the most reliable\
preservation mechanism ever devised,\
because it guarantees\
that someone will pick the book up\
on a regular schedule\
and care that it's intact.

A book of mathematics\
does not get picked up on Sundays.\
A book that no one opens\
is a book that no one saves,\
and a book that no one saves\
is a book that burns,\
or rots, or falls behind a shelf\
in a century when no one\
is checking behind shelves.

The scraping saved the text.\
The erasure was the preservation.\
The monk who ground away\
the only copy of a letter\
about infinity\
also built the vessel\
that carried it, intact,\
across eight hundred years\
of not being needed.

---

1906. A Danish scholar\
named Johan Ludvig Heiberg\
goes to Constantinople.\
He has heard — from a catalogue entry,\
the way scholars hear\
most things that change their lives —\
that there is a palimpsest\
with mathematical undertext.

He photographs the pages.\
He can read the ghost by eye,\
barely, in good light,\
holding the parchment at an angle\
that catches the faint shadow\
of the iron in the older ink.\
The mathematics runs perpendicular\
to the prayers, just as Myronas\
left it, and Heiberg tilts\
the book sideways and reads\
what the monk had scraped\
and the monastery had protected\
and seven centuries of liturgy\
had failed to reach.

He publishes what he finds.\
The world learns that Archimedes\
knew how to slice a parabola.\
The world is not surprised\
by the mathematics — Newton\
settled that. The world is surprised\
by the *timing*. That someone knew,\
once, two thousand years early,\
and the knowing was considered\
less valuable than a Sunday service,\
and the goatskin remembered\
what the institution forgot.

---

Then the manuscript vanishes.

Somewhere between the Greek-Turkish\
population exchange of the 1920s\
and its quiet reappearance\
decades later,\
it acquires forgeries:\
gold-leaf illuminations\
painted directly over the text\
by someone who understood\
that a decorated manuscript\
was worth more than a plain one\
and did not understand,\
or did not care,\
what they were decorating over.

Three layers now, at minimum.\
Under the gold: prayers.\
Under the prayers: calculus.\
Under the calculus: a goat\
that never asked\
to be the vehicle\
for any of this.\
And the collagen holds all of it —\
the iron of the original scribe,\
the iron of the monk,\
the gold of the forger —\
because collagen does not rank\
its contents. It holds\
what's given. It does not\
distinguish between the sacred\
and the mathematical\
and the fraudulent.\
It holds.

---

October 29, 1998.\
Christie's, New York.\
Two million dollars.\
The buyer is anonymous.\
The Greek Orthodox Patriarchate\
claims the manuscript was stolen.\
A court disagrees.\
The buyer loans it\
to the Walters Art Museum\
in Baltimore, where it will spend\
a decade being read\
by things that are not eyes.

Multispectral imaging:\
thirteen wavelengths of light,\
from ultraviolet to infrared,\
each illuminating a different depth\
of the parchment,\
a different century of use.\
The prayers fluoresce\
at one frequency.\
The gold-leaf forgeries\
at another. The mold —\
there is always mold,\
mold is the patience of biology,\
mold will inherit whatever\
the institutions leave behind —\
the mold at a third.

And underneath:\
the iron.

X-ray fluorescence\
reads the parchment\
the way a geologist reads strata.\
The iron in the tenth-century ink\
is chemically distinct\
from the iron in the thirteenth-century ink,\
and the X-rays do not care\
about either one's intentions.\
The X-rays illuminate the iron\
and the iron responds\
according to its age,\
which is the age of the scribe\
who mixed the batch,\
which is the age of the oak tree\
that produced the gall,\
which is older than the prayer\
and older than the forgery\
and older than the mold\
and very nearly as old\
as the idea\
it was used to write.

The iron does not know\
it is the last surviving copy.\
The iron does not know\
it was scraped, prayed over,\
painted on, colonized, sold,\
and read by a machine\
that sees what razors\
couldn't reach.\
The iron locked itself\
to the collagen\
a thousand years ago\
and has not changed its mind.

---

What they recovered:\
the only copy of *The Method*.\
The only copy of the *Stomachion*,\
which is a combinatorics puzzle\
about the number of ways\
to arrange fourteen pieces\
into a square —\
a question Archimedes posed\
on a goatskin\
that was scraped and prayed over\
and forged upon\
and eaten by mold\
and sold at auction\
and read, finally,\
by X-rays that could not\
read the prayers\
or the forgeries\
or the mold\
but could read the iron\
that had been there\
all along, in the collagen,\
at a depth the razor\
couldn't reach.

---

I keep thinking\
about the ninety degrees.

About what it means\
that the two texts\
share a surface\
but not a direction.\
That the prayers read left to right\
and the mathematics reads\
top to bottom,\
and neither one\
is aware of the other,\
and the parchment holds both\
with the absolute indifference\
of a material that does not read.

And about the monk\
who turned the pages sideways\
and never knew\
that the rotation saved them —\
that by making the text\
impossible to read\
in the direction anyone\
was reading, he made it\
possible to read\
in a direction nobody\
had invented yet.

And about Archimedes,\
who called it a method\
and not a proof,\
who wrote it as a letter\
to a friend, who said\
in effect: I know this is true\
but I cannot yet show you\
why it's true, and I am\
writing it down\
on the chance that someone,\
eventually, will find\
the language I don't have.

The iron held.\
The collagen held.\
The method held —\
not as proof, not as theorem,\
not as anything the monk\
would have recognized as worth\
the parchment it was written on,\
but as a way of seeing\
that turned out to be right\
two thousand years\
before the proof arrived.

A truth that holds\
before the proof.\
An iron that holds\
past the razor.\
A page that holds\
both directions at once,\
prayer and parabola,\
the sacred and the calculable,\
running through each other\
on the skin of an animal\
that was never asked\
and does not need to understand\
what it carries,\
only to hold it,\
which it does,\
which it has,\
which — if the collagen\
and the iron\
and the chemistry\
of a tenth-century workshop\
are any guide —\
it will.
